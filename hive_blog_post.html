Blog post / design note

From a bare hex grid to a spatial science sandbox

This upgraded simulator turns a simple hex‑map prototype into a richer research and communication tool. It now behaves more like an exploratory lab: it has scenario presets, reproducible randomness, ecological and evolutionary dynamics, multiple linked diagnostics, and a layout that feels ready for a scientific web product instead of just a proof of concept.

The result is not just prettier. It is more legible, more hypothesis‑friendly, and much better at showing why a spatially explicit model matters when fragmentation, disturbance, dispersal, and adaptation interact.

What changed

The redesign focused on three goals at once: make the page easier to read, make the controls scientifically interpretable, and make the outcomes visible through views that answer real spatial questions.

01

Layered map views

The landscape can now be viewed as population load, trait mean, habitat quality, environmental optimum, trait mismatch, or disturbance intensity. That means the same grid can answer different questions without changing the underlying simulation.

02

Scientifically meaningful metrics

The dashboard tracks total abundance, occupancy, invasion front radius, mean trait, adaptation debt, trait diversity, and patchiness. Those are much more informative than a single map snapshot.

03

Linked diagnostics

Alongside the map, the interface now shows time series, trait histograms, radial profiles, and per‑step fluxes. This turns the simulator into a compact multi‑panel figure generator.

04

Disturbance and fragmentation

Patch disturbances and hard barriers make the landscape more realistic and more interesting. Users can inspect resilience, recolonization, refugia, and the cost of broken connectivity.

05

Adaptive dynamics

The simulator now includes local optima, mutational noise, adaptive tracking, and selection against mismatch. That makes it possible to explore evolutionary rescue and lag under moving environments.

06

Preset stories and reproducibility

Preset scenarios make the app immediately demo‑ready, while an explicit random seed makes it much easier to reproduce and compare runs.

Model logic in one glance

The new version keeps the model exploratory, but gives it a clearer ecological backbone. Population, environment, and trait state each have a visual role and a dynamical role.

At a high level, each hex stores a population size N, a mean trait φ, habitat quality H, an environmental optimum θ, and a disturbance level. Population changes through births, deaths, emigration, and immigration. Traits shift through adaptation, mutation, drift, and weak coupling to neighboring patches.

Local demography

Nᵢ(t+1) = Nᵢ(t) + Bᵢ − Dᵢ + Iᵢ − Eᵢ

Births are limited by local crowding and niche mismatch. Deaths increase with disturbance and overcrowding. Dispersal moves individuals through the hex neighborhood.

Adaptive tracking

φᵢ(t+1) = φᵢ(t) + α(θᵢ − φᵢ) + εmut + εdrift

Trait dynamics let the simulation show adaptation debt, tracking of moving optima, and the interaction between mutation and spatial spread.

Habitat structure

Kᵢ = K₀ · Hᵢ

Carrying capacity varies with habitat quality, which means the landscape matters even before any disturbance or climate shift is applied.

A useful design rule here is that every latent variable should either influence a decision, appear in a tooltip, or earn a chart. Otherwise it becomes hidden complexity instead of visible science.

What the visuals are good for

The new interface is built so that each panel answers a different kind of question.

How fast is the spread?

Use the normalized demography chart plus the invasion front metric. Together they separate local growth from genuine spatial expansion.

Is the population adapting?

Use the evolutionary tracking chart and the mismatch layer. The gap between trait mean and optimum shows adaptation lag immediately.

Where is the wave front?

Use the radial profile. It shows whether abundance is concentrated near the seed, spread smoothly, or piling up behind barriers and bottlenecks.

Is diversity collapsing?

Use the trait histogram and diversity metric to see whether a scenario produces convergence, broad polymorphism, or fragmented local niches.

Are disturbances changing outcomes?

Use the disturbance layer together with the flux panel. This makes it easier to distinguish internal decline from externally forced loss.

Are corridors actually helping?

Use the corridor preset, then compare occupancy, patchiness, and migrants. The answer becomes visual instead of abstract.

Why this matters for a risk platform

A spatial sandbox like this complements network dashboards and timeseries views because it lets users reason about mechanisms, not just summaries.

If your broader platform is about risk assessment, this kind of page plays a distinct role. A trade network view tells you where connections exist. A timeseries tells you what changed over time. A spatial simulator helps users think about why a pattern might emerge under different assumptions.

That makes it valuable for communication, training, scenario exploration, and early prototyping of intervention logic before a more formal model is fitted.

For science communication Show how fragmentation, barriers, or adaptation can change spread trajectories.
For stakeholder workshops Use presets to compare alternative futures and discuss where interventions make sense.
For product design Learn which metrics and map layers users naturally reach for before investing in more complex tooling.

Where to take it next

The current version is a strong interactive demo. The next step is to decide whether you want it to become a communication piece, a teaching tool, or a calibrated modeling workflow.

Empirical calibration

Swap generic habitat and optimum fields for measured covariates or gridded risk surfaces. Then use the same visual grammar for real scenarios.

Intervention experiments

Add barrier removal, targeted suppression, vaccination, culling, or corridor construction as explicit actions that can be toggled during a run.

Network + space coupling

Link the spatial layer to movement or contact networks so users can explore what happens when jump dispersal and local diffusion coexist.
